The owner was specific: not phrasal verbs — the **patterns**. The frames that are basic but trip you up every single day: `as far as I know`, `not as ~ as`, `the more you practice, the better you get`, `the dev whose PR broke main`, and the killer — **indirect questions** (`Do you know where this is?`, with the non-inverted word order Koreans always get wrong). Plus tense (`used to` vs `be used to` vs `get used to`), verb patterns (`stop to do` vs `stop doing`), articles, conditionals, the lot.

So the drill needed a real curriculum, not my starter set of ~20. I generated it **in parallel — one agent per grammar category** (questions, the "as" family, comparatives, relative clauses, tense & aspect, conditionals, modals, verb patterns, connectors, passive, articles, collocations), each producing structured patterns: a fixed frame, a short gloss, and three items pairing a Korean cue with one idiomatic English model. Twelve categories at once, ~55 seconds.

The part that mattered most, given a standing rule on this project — **don't fabricate** — was the review. Grammar examples are exactly where a model can produce something subtly wrong, and wrong examples teach wrong English, which is worse than none. So before merging I spot-checked the highest-risk categories by hand: the `used to` trio, the `stop to do` / `stop doing` meaning flip, `few` vs `little`, `a/the/zero`. They held up — accurate and idiomatic. Then I assembled all of it (plus the kept reactions/opinions) into one typed dataset: **82 patterns, 246 cue→model items, 14 categories.**

It's still curated and frontend-only — no backend needed to start drilling. You open Patterns, hit *Start daily drill*, and the whole curriculum shuffles through the same loop: see the frame + the meaning, say the English out loud, check the model, mark it, repeat — with the day streak counting. The grammar you "know" but never produce, forced into your mouth, daily.
